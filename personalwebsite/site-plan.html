Hi, I'm Seth Roberts, a software engineering student at BYU-Idaho. I've been coding for about a year and have found a real passion for it. I'm a fast learner who enjoys thinking through problems and planning out new ideas. While I'm still building my toolkit, I'm focused on mastering the fundamentals and making the most of what I know.

About Me

When I'm not coding, you'll most likely find me outside. Whether it's carving down fresh powder on a ski run or setting up my hammock in the canyon, I love being in nature. Any chance to gather around a campfire with family and friends is time well spent. The outdoors is where I recharge, find inspiration, and embrace new adventures.

I fell in love with filmmaking in high school, competing in video contests with my friends and learning tools like Premiere Pro, After Effects, and Photoshop. Since then, I've kept that creative spark alive by exploring programs like Unity, Blender, and Godot. I'm always excited to learn new tools and see what I can create.

I grew up designing homes with my dad's company, putting my name on everything from cozy cabins to custom homes and poolhouses. Designing spaces has always been something I love, and it's a skill I know I'll carry with me for life.

I love my family! There is nothing that makes me more happy than my family, and I love to spend time hanging out with them and playing games or spending time outside.

Work Experience

With a strong foundation in drafting and site surveying, Seth Roberts has spent years ensuring precision in architectural plans while optimizing workflows with AutoCAD tools. At Peak Home Design, he developed custom drafting solutions that significantly increased efficiency and accuracy. His ability to communicate complex design concepts clearly to clients ensured seamless project execution.

Beyond design, his experience as a Pharmacy Technician Trainee at Walgreens sharpened his attention to detail and problem-solving skills, as he quickly became one of the most efficient employees. His time in door-to-door sales further refined his adaptability, customer interaction, and negotiation skills in fast-paced environments.

I have worked a couple of jobs that I think are important to note. Starting with my career in residential drafting and design, I grew up working with my dad since I was 14. I learned AutoCad when I was even younger, but as I grew older I began to take on real projects. When I turned 14, I began to design real plans. I became fluent with AutoCad and specifically became very interested in how creating “dynamic blocks” could speed up the design process.

I found new tools to help my dad's business in the field measuring aspects as well as by outlining processes in the field and creating organized Excel sheets for tracking expenses, incomes, and time spent on different phases of projects in order to give more accurate bids.

In two different summers I have done summer sales jobs. Going out and knocking on doors with a sales pitch to get sales was not something I would have expected myself to do when I was younger. The time I spent memorizing pitches and fine-tuning my body language skills has been invaluable to me. I learned confidence, resilience, and how to be comfortable in uncomfortable situations. I learned accountability. A lot of your success depended upon how well you could shape your mindset, and how well you prepared yourself and trained.

At Walgreens Pharmacy I was a pharmacy technician. It was here that I first realized my love for learning. This was a very fast-paced environment and I felt a lot was expected of me right from the start. I had to memorize a huge list of drugs with different strengths and effects, I had to learn a new program for writing and deciphering prescriptions, and I had to memorize many different systems that insurances would use. Memorizing the insurances was likely one of the more difficult parts.

It seemed that you could never just guess what problems insurances would have with different drugs or prescriptions; it was problem by problem. I actively threw myself in front of the customers more than I needed to because I wanted to feel confident in my space. I knew this would only come through failure after failure. The more times a prescription had a problem, the more solutions I could become familiar with.

I quickly grew in my position and came to the point where people who had worked for months were asking me for help in only my third week there. It was through this experience that I learned an active learning environment engages me very strongly, and that was what piqued my interest in code.
